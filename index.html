the opener
ask a question in the first 10 seconds. not a pitch, not an intro — a question. everything flows from their answer.
never say
"hi, my name is chris and i'm calling from blaztly. we're a website company and i was wondering if you had a few minutes to talk about your online presence..."
signals sales call immediately. they shut down in 3 seconds.
say this
"hey [name], this is chris — quick question for you." [pause 1 second] "i looked up [business name] and noticed [scraped observation]. are you the right person to talk to about that?"
why it works
the pause forces them to respond before you've said anything that sounds like a sales call. "are you the right person" is disarming — most people say yes just to find out what you noticed.
once they confirm — the hook
"the reason i'm calling is we help local businesses in the area get a custom site up in under a week — one that actually shows up when people are searching for what you do. i just want to ask you one quick question."
"one quick question" is your permission bridge. they almost always say go ahead. now you're in discovery.
discovery
ask 1 to 3 questions, then stop. don't pitch here. the more they talk, the more they talk themselves into having a problem.
1
awareness — ask this every time
"when someone searches for [their service] in [city], do you know where you show up?"
most say no — that's your opening
2
source of business — if they engage
"are most of your new customers from referrals, or do you get any from online?"
all referrals = a ceiling on their growth
3
gut check — if they're skeptical
"if a potential customer googled your business right now — what would they find?"
let them answer. silence is fine.
rule
do not pitch during discovery. just ask and listen. the pivot comes after.
the pivot
one result, one sentence. connect what they just told you to why the meeting is worth 20 minutes.
say this
"that's exactly why i reached out. we just launched a site for a local landscaper in the area — amigolandscaping.com — and they started getting inbound calls from google within the first month. i'd love to show you what that could look like for your business."
use the real example
amigolandscaping.com is real. if their business type is different, swap "landscaper" for the right one and keep everything else. the story is: real client, real result, google calls within a month.
never do this
"we offer custom websites, mobile-responsive design, seo optimization, google business integration, ssl certificates, hosting, unlimited edits, and lite branding..."
one outcome beats ten features. always.
the close
close for the meeting, not the sale. low pressure, two specific days, nothing to decide except a time slot.
say this
"i'm not trying to sell you anything today — i just want to spend 20 minutes showing you what your online presence looks like from a customer's point of view, and what it could look like. do you have time thursday or friday this week?"
two-choice close
always name two specific days — never "when are you free?" if neither works, ask them to name a day. don't end without a specific time on the calendar.
once they say yes
"perfect — what's your email? i'll send the calendar invite right now."
send it during the call. confirmed invites show 3× higher attendance than verbal agreements.
close rules
stop talking the moment they say yes
never give pricing on a cold call
the booked zoom is the win — log it
no-show? follow up once by text that same day
objections
every objection is a timing or trust issue. remove the friction, don't win the argument.
"i already have a website"▼
"perfect. quick question — when someone finds it on their phone, does it actually load fast and show your services clearly? a lot of businesses have a site but it's not bringing them calls. that's exactly what i'd show you."
pivot to quality, not existence. a bad site often ranks worse than no site.
"not interested"▼
"totally fair. real quick — do you get any customers from google right now, or is it mostly word of mouth?"
don't argue. just ask a discovery question. if they answer, you're back in. if they hang up, they weren't a buyer.
"how much does it cost?"▼
"i don't want to throw a number at you before i understand your situation — every business is different. that's what the 20 minutes is for. does thursday work?"
if they absolutely won't move: "setup is $700 one-time, $39 a month for hosting — everything included. but let me show you the full picture first." then re-close immediately.
"just send me an email"▼
"happy to. before i do — quick question so i send you something relevant: are you the person who makes decisions on marketing and your online presence?"
qualify them before spending time on an email. if they confirm, you've also re-confirmed the decision maker.
"i don't have time right now"▼
"completely get it — that's why i'm calling instead of stopping by. it's 20 minutes on zoom, i come to you. would early morning or late afternoon work better this week?"
"i tried a website before and it didn't work"▼
"that's really common — most of those sites weren't built to show up on google or turn a visitor into a call. that's the whole difference in what we do. 20 minutes and you'll see exactly why."
validate the bad experience, then differentiate. you're not asking them to trust you — just to look.
"i use social media instead"▼
"that's smart — social is great for people who already know you. google catches people who've never heard of you but are actively searching for what you do right now. those are your hottest leads. i'd love to show you."
don't compete with social — complement it. then re-close for the meeting.
