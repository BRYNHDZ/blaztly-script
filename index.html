playbook · pre-built site flow
the flow: pre-build the site → cold call to book a 5-min reveal 24+ hrs out → google meet walkthrough → show, then price, then close. unsold sites become portfolio pieces.
pre-call setup
the scraper qualifies. your job is to show up knowing one specific thing about them and knowing what site you're going to build (or have already built).
what the scraper surfaces
no websitenot mobile-friendlyno google business listing 0 reviewssite loads slowno booking button site outdated 3+ yrscompetitor outranking themsocial only, no site
1
read the scraper observation
that one specific thing drives the build and gets name-dropped during the reveal. know it before you dial.
2
know their business type
restaurant vs. service (landscaper, barber, contractor). shapes the site you build.
3
identify the decision maker
owner or manager. gatekeeper branch is handled on the cold call — you still want to know who you're trying to reach.
know the offer
setup (one-time)$700 — april discount, bumps after april 30
hosting + support$39/mo
live in≤ 7 days from paid
includesdomain, hosting, ssl, branding, seo, netlify email form, unlimited small edits
custom tierextra pages, crm, ordering integrations — separate quote
real client exampleamigolandscaping.com
build sop — what goes in the base tier
four sections, one page (or a simple multi-page for service businesses with distinct offerings). keep it tight. custom work is upsell.
1
hero
business name + one-line value prop + a cta (call or contact form).
2
services or menu
swap based on business type. pull copy from their google business profile or social.
3
about / photos
short paragraph + images sourced from their google business or socials.
4
contact — netlify email form
submissions hit their inbox directly. mention this during the reveal walkthrough.
tie the build to the scraper observation
one deliberate choice that shows you did your homework:
  • "not mobile-friendly" → aggressively mobile-first. open the reveal on your phone.
  • "no google business" → hours / location / map section front and center.
  • "site outdated" → modern minimal aesthetic, emphasize load speed.
  • "no booking button" → prominent cta as the hero action.
do not build on spec
crm hooks, online ordering, custom domain email, blog, multi-language, animations beyond basics. all upsell — never free.
when to pre-build vs. build in the gap
high-probability leads (active google business, visibly bad site, clearly has budget) — pre-build before the call. cold unknowns — build after booking during the 24-hr gap. either way, the cold call always treats the site as already made.
restaurant ordering — quick ref
common platforms: toast, square for restaurants, clover, chownow. you don't build — embed their widget or link out. if they don't have one, recommend chownow or square. upsell conversation, not base tier.
the cold call
30 to 60 seconds. one flow, four beats. this is a booking call — not a sales call. goal = a booked google meet with an email attached. nothing else counts.
1
opener
no intro, no company name. lead with the deliverable. the pattern interrupt is the whole point — most owners hear this and freeze for half a second because it doesn't fit any cold-call template they've heard before.
natural version"hey, our team made you guys a website. are you the right person to talk to about that? i was hoping to show someone something like that."
2
gatekeeper branch — only if they're not the decision maker
don't pitch the gatekeeper. get a name + a callback window. move on.
natural version"no worries — who's the right person? any chance they're around right now?"
3
close — once they confirm
two specific times, both 24+ hrs out. "kind of busy today" justifies the gap AND plants the "in demand" frame. if neither slot works, ask them to pick a day — don't end without a specific time.
natural version"cool — it'll take 5 minutes to walk you through it. we're kind of busy today — how does tomorrow around 10 or 3 work?"
4
book it — during the call
calendar invite goes out while you're still on the phone. blaztly.com link goes in the invite description — that's their trust buffer during the 24-hr gap.
natural version"perfect — what's your email? i'll send the google meet invite now. i'll throw in blaztly.com too so you can see what we do in the meantime."
cold call rules
stop talking the second they say yes to a time
never say a price on the cold call — defer to the reveal
"just send me the link" is in objections — use the scripted answer
no booked meet = no win. log the call and move on
scripts are a flow, not a read — adapt wording, keep the shape
the gap
the 24+ hrs between booking and reveal. your build window, your perception play, your trust buffer. treating it as dead space is how you lose deals between booking and reveal.
immediate post-call (within 5 minutes of hanging up)
1
send the google meet invite
title: "blaztly x [business name] — quick site walkthrough (5 min)". send it now, not later.
2
include blaztly.com in the invite
in the description: "here's what we do — have a look when you get a chance." one line, no hard sell.
3
optional follow-up text (~30 min later)
"sent the invite + our site. see you [time]." keeps you top of mind, doesn't feel pushy.
the build
this is the build window
if the site isn't pre-built, build it now. follow the base-tier sop from the pre-call tab: hero, services/menu, about/photos, netlify contact form. one deliberate choice tied to the scraper observation. don't over-build — base tier is base tier.
gap rules — protecting the window
"can you send the link now?"
→
"not live yet — hosting costs money before you've committed. i'll screen-share on our call."
"can we move it up?"
→
"let me check — we have a few of these booked this week, but i'll see what i can do."
hard rule
never move the meeting closer than ~18 hours out. you need the build buffer, and too-soon availability breaks the "busy" frame.
the reveal
5 minutes on google meet. five beats. show → their reaction → price → close. this is where the money decision happens.
1
warm-up
~30 sec
small talk while they join. confirm they can see your screen. no pitch.
2
walkthrough
~2–3 min
share screen. load their site. walk top to bottom, narrate lightly — don't over-explain.
  • hero → services/menu → about/photos → contact form
  • on the form: "this hits your inbox directly."
  • if scraper observation was mobile-related, flip to phone view — that's the "oh wow" moment.
drop the differentiator once, naturally"this is hand-coded — ai-assisted, but not a template. not wordpress, not wix. means you can customize literally anything down the line."
3
their reaction
~30 sec
stop narrating. ask. let them talk. this is the single most important moment of the call.
natural version"so — what do you think?"
if they like it: go to beat 4.
if they're lukewarm: "what would you change?" small changes (colors, copy, photos) are free, base tier. big changes (new sections, integrations) are custom — flag it, don't price it mid-reveal.
4
price
~30 sec
deliver the number. then shut up. first person to talk after the number loses.
natural version"here's how it works — starts at $700 right now, that's our april discount. $39 a month covers hosting, domain, ssl, and unlimited small edits. custom work — extra pages, ordering, crm — that's a separate conversation. but what's on your screen right now? $700."
5
close
~1 min
four branches. pick the one that matches their reaction.
yes
"perfect. i'll send a payment link — once that's settled, we push it live within 7 days. what email should the invoice go to?"
"let me think about it"
"totally fair. heads up — the $700 discount ends april 30, bumps up after that. want me to hold the rate until [date before april 30]?"
soft deadline without pushing. gets a commit date without closing them live.
"i want to change x, y, z" (upsell)
"cool — let me put together a quick quote for the custom work and i'll send it over tonight. sound good?"
never price custom live. promise a quote. send after.
no / not now — portfolio back-pocket
"no worries. mind if i keep you on our list? if this works for someone else as a portfolio piece, i'll let you know."
leaves the door open. reinforces that we don't need the sale.
reveal rules
show before you tell the price — always in that order
once the price is out, stop talking
never price custom work live — promise a quote, send after
"why is it $700?" → "because we hand-code everything. not a template." one line, back to the close
the portfolio line is a back-pocket — deploy once, don't lean on it
objections
every objection is a timing or trust issue. the right answer depends on which call you're on — split below.
on the cold call
"just send me the link"▼
"can't — it's not live yet. custom coded, and hosting it before you've committed costs us money. i'll screen-share it on google meet — takes 5 minutes."
this is the single most important cold-call objection to get right. do not freelance it.
"how much is it?"▼
"i'd rather show you first — takes 5 minutes. once you see it, i'll walk you through the numbers. tomorrow 10 or 3?"
no price on the cold call. ever. defer to the reveal.
"why did you build me a site without asking?"▼
"honestly? we build these for local businesses we think look promising. if you love it, great. if not, we keep it as an example of our work. no pressure either way."
the portfolio frame. disarms the "why me?" without admitting we need the sale.
"i already have a website"▼
"cool — we looked at it. when's the last time someone found you through google vs. someone you already knew? that's what the 5 minutes is for."
pivot to quality, not existence. a bad site often ranks worse than no site.
"not interested"▼
"totally fair. one second — do you get any customers from google searches, or mostly word of mouth?"
don't argue. ask a discovery question. if they answer, you're back in. if they hang up, they weren't a buyer.
"i'll think about it / call me back"▼
"sure — but i won't have these slots open next week. tomorrow 10 or 3 — pick one and i'll hold it. if it's bad, you cancel."
scarcity + reversibility. gives them an easy out, which makes saying yes cheaper.
on the reveal
"it's too expensive"▼
"$700 is the april rate — goes up may 1. for context, that's what you'd pay for a wix template someone set up in an afternoon. this is hand-coded, yours forever, live in 7 days."
anchor against templates, not against nothing.
"can you do it cheaper?"▼
"the $700 is already the discount. what i can do is hold the rate past april 30 if you commit today."
don't drop the price. trade a deadline extension instead.
"i want to show my partner / spouse / business partner"▼
"of course. want to loop them in right now on this call? otherwise i'll send a recording — when can you two talk it over?"
get a specific follow-up time. "i'll let you know" is not an answer.
"what if i don't like the edits i want?"▼
"small stuff — colors, copy, photos — unlimited edits are included. big stuff — new sections, integrations — that's a custom quote, but you're not locked in. we do that conversation after you're live."
make it safe to say yes. custom is not a commitment.
"what happens if i cancel hosting later?"▼
"site comes down. you own the code — we can hand it off. most people stay because $39 covers hosting, domain, ssl, and edits — hard to beat."
honesty + reframe to why staying is the easier choice.
